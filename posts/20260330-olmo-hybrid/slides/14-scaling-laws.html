Why Expressivity Improves Scaling

Adapting the quantization model of neural scaling laws

The idea

Model language modeling as learning many discrete "tasks" (quanta), each with a threshold of data needed. Loss follows a Chinchilla-style power law in parameters $N$ and tokens $D$:

$L(N, D) = E_\epsilon + \frac{A_\epsilon}{N^\alpha} + \frac{B_\epsilon}{D^\beta}$

The paper adds expressibility: each task is either expressible (prob $1-\epsilon$) or not. For inexpressible tasks, the model needs more data/params to approximate.

The subscripts on $E_\epsilon$, $A_\epsilon$, $B_\epsilon$ mean these constants depend on architecture via $\epsilon$. Lower $\epsilon$ (more expressive) → lower constants → loss curve shifts down.

Crucially: the exponents $\alpha, \beta$ don't change. Only the constants shift.

* "Language modeling = many discrete tasks": Michaud et al. 2023, NeurIPS.

Empirical validation

Chinchilla fits across 7 sizes (60M to 1B):

  • Data coefficient $B_\epsilon$: 83.7 (hybrid) vs 94.9 (transformer). Non-overlapping 95% CIs. Each token is worth more to the hybrid.
  • Scaling exponents $\alpha, \beta$: indistinguishable across architectures (as theory predicts).
  • Extrapolation to 7B: predictions match actual loss within <0.3% error.

Projected token savings

~1.3x at 1B → ~1.7x at 7B → ~1.9x at 70B

Savings grow with scale because the data coefficient $B_\epsilon$ dominates at large $D$.
