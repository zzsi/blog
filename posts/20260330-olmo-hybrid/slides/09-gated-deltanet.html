Step 4: Negative Eigenvalues

Grazzi et al., ICLR 2025. Unlocking state tracking.

One small change

Replace $\beta_t$ with $\mathbf{2}\beta_t$ in the erasure term:

$S_t = S_{t-1} \cdot \alpha_t(I - \color{#E41159}{\mathbf{2}}\beta_t k_t k_t^\top) + v_t k_t^\top$

When $\beta_t > 0.5$, the matrix $(I - 2\beta_t k_t k_t^\top)$ has a negative eigenvalue along $k_t$.

The state component in that direction flips sign rather than just shrinking.

This is the version OLMo Hybrid uses. The difference between "can represent state tracking" and "cannot" is binary, not incremental.

Concrete example: parity tracking

Task: reading a bit sequence, is the running count of 1s even or odd?

Without negative eigenvalues:

State can only decay toward zero. It can shrink, but it can't oscillate. It literally cannot represent a toggle.

With negative eigenvalues:

State along $k_t$ can flip:
$+1 \to -1 \to +1 \to \ldots$

Now it can track parity, simulate counters, follow finite automata.

This is why OLMo Hybrid uses GDN+neg eigenvalues rather than standard GDN (used by Qwen 3.5) or Mamba-2 (used by Nemotron-H).

* Grazzi et al. prove that GDN with negative eigenvalues can simulate arbitrary finite automata.
